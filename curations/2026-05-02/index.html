What Comes After

Endings, evidence, and the strange art of beginning again

Spirit Airlines flies its last flight. A racing champion who defied two life-ending accidents finally rests. ChatGPT conversations become courtroom exhibits. And in Italy, wheels of cheese sit in bank vaults, appreciating like fine art.

Meanwhile, China's electric vehicles quietly reshape the century while everyone argues about oil.

Endings aren't really endings—they're the spaces where something else begins. Today's artists know this.

Business

Spirit Airlines Cancels Everything—It's Over

The ultra-low-cost carrier that made flying feel like a bus ride is done. Spirit canceled all flights, halting operations permanently. An empire built on bare-bones service and yellow planes, now just static. Nonceptualism's "The King of the World" captures the energy perfectly: a fractured sovereign assembled from corrupted data and half-remembered machinery. His face is less a visage than a collapsing signal. Every empire ends. The question is what signal remains.

by Nonceptualism

"The King of the World rises as a fractured sovereign—an empire of glitches, bone-white static, and metallic ruin. His face is less a visage than a collapsing signal."

Tech

Your ChatGPT History Is Now Evidence

Prosecutors have discovered something useful: people confess to AI. ChatGPT conversations are becoming courtroom evidence, a "treasure trove" of admissions, plans, and digital breadcrumbs. We talk to machines like they won't talk back. ooakosimo's interactive piece "Critique & Complicity Overlap" hits this precisely: "This machine critiques capitalism while actively participating in it... The machine watches back." Every prompt is a confession. Every response, a witness.

Sports

Alex Zanardi, 1966–2026

The Italian racing driver who lost his legs in a 2001 crash, then won four Paralympic gold medals in handcycling, then survived another devastating accident in 2020—Alex Zanardi has died at 59. His story was never about the crashes. It was about what came after them. ForkForest's "The afterfire" was photographed three years after a human-set fire ravaged an Argentine forest. Nature finding a way back. "We made a small reforestation... Stepped back." Some things don't end. They transform.

The afterfire

by ForkForest

"This picture was taken three years after a fire—set by a human—expanded over the forest near El Trigal school. Nature invites us to observe a way back."

Economy

What Oil Crisis? China's EVs Are Already Winning

While the West debates fossil fuels and sanctions, China quietly crossed a threshold: electric vehicles are now the default, not the alternative. The 21st century's dominant transport technology is being built in Shenzhen, not Detroit. 3spiral's "Síntese" emerged from a similar transformation—organic fragments isolated from forest photography, transformed into digital stickers, made into something new. "From these records, elements of the forest were isolated and transformed." Old inputs, new synthesis.

Culture

Inside Italy's Secret Cheese Bank

In Emilia-Romagna, banks accept wheels of Parmigiano Reggiano as collateral. 400,000 wheels sit in climate-controlled vaults, aging into value. It sounds absurd until you remember: all value is agreed-upon fiction. Currency, stocks, NFTs—we decide what matters and then act like we discovered it. TOCA ME's "7-inch / bonustrack" plays with the same energy: a GIF as record, a bonus track that exists outside the album, value where you least expect it.
